**1.** **Introduction**



a b s t r a c t


Modern vehicles depend on the Controller Area Network (CAN) for electronic control unit (ECU) communication,
but its inherent vulnerabilities necessitate robust intrusion detection systems (IDS). Current machine learning
and deep learning IDS solutions struggle with limited labeled data, class imbalances, and costly data collection
processes. Few-shot learning, effective with few labeled samples, remains underexplored for in-vehicle networks
(IVNs) despite its potential in data-scarce automotive cybersecurity scenarios. To bridge this gap, we introduce
the first few-shot learning approach for multi-class intrusion detection in IVNs, leveraging a novel, lightweight
Convolutional Anomaly Transformer. By integrating a 1D convolutional layer with an Anomaly Transformer,
our model effectively classifies diverse attack types with minimal training data, mitigating class imbalance.
Experiments on the widely-used real-world Car Hacking dataset, the complex ROAD dataset, and the distinct
CAN-ML dataset validate its efficacy. On the Car Hacking dataset, we achieve an exceptional F1 score of 0.9994
with only 2 % of training data, improving to 0.9999 with 10 %. On the challenging ROAD dataset, characterized
by diverse attacks and high variability, the model achieves an F1 score of up to 0.9980 using just 10 % of training
data. Demonstrating strong generalization capabilities, the model also attains an impressive F1 score of 0.9918
on the CAN-ML dataset, which features entirely different vehicles and attack distributions. Furthermore, the
lightweight architecture of our proposed IDS enables practical deployment in resource-constrained automotive

environments.



Modern vehicles are complex cyber-physical systems equipped with
advanced technologies like blind spot monitoring, lane departure warnings, and automatic emergency braking, all of which enhance safety and
efficiency [1]. These systems rely on electronic control units (ECUs) that
communicate over the Controller Area Network (CAN) [2], facilitating
real-time data exchange between critical components. However, despite
its crucial role, the CAN bus protocol has inherent security vulnerabilities. These vulnerabilities leave vehicles susceptible to various cyberattacks that could disable essential functions such as braking or engine
control [3–6].

Securing in-vehicle networks (IVNs) has progressed beyond static
methods such as cryptography and firewalls, which struggle against dynamic threats [3]. In response, researchers turned to machine learn


ing and later, more potent deep learning architectures like CNNs and
Transformers, to build adaptive intrusion detection systems (IDSs) [7,8].
While these models achieve high detection rates, their performance
hinges on being trained with extensive, pre-labeled datasets. This reliance on large-scale labeled data hinders defense against novel attacks
and complicates IDS adaptation as new threats emerge. A similar trend
appears in related domains, such as the Industrial Internet of Things,
where researchers have recently employed advanced approaches such
as evolutionary adversarial autoencoders [9] and automated neural architecture search [10] for robust anomaly detection. While these stateof-the-art methods highlight the power of modern deep learning, their
direct applicability to IVNs is not straightforward due to the unique constraints of the CAN protocol and real-time ECU processing. Furthermore,
despite advances in architectural complexity, two significant shortcomings in the IVN context still need to be addressed.

Firstly, most research has focused on binary classification, categorizing data as either normal or abnormal [11–14]. However, binary
classification models cannot classify different types of attacks, which is
crucial for understanding the threat landscape and implementing precise
countermeasures. Without the ability to classify different attack types,
response strategies remain generic, hence less effective. Therefore, this
study focuses on solving the multi-class classification problem, which
is essential for accurately detecting and differentiating various attack
types in CAN networks.

However, transitioning from binary to multi-class classification introduces distinct challenges. A multi-class model faces the challenge
of distinguishing between highly similar attacks, such as Gear Spoofing versus RPM Spoofing. This requirement increases data scarcity since
each attack class contains far fewer examples than the already limited
pool of attack data overall. This trade-off pits the need for more complex models capable of learning multiple decision boundaries against
the strict resource constraints of automotive ECUs.


Secondly, all supervised deep learning-based IDSs face the overarching challenge of requiring extensive labeled datasets for high accuracy.
In real-world automotive environments, collecting and labeling a wide
variety of attack samples is costly and often impractical, resulting in
datasets where benign data vastly outnumbers the few available abnormal samples.

Anomaly Transformer as a powerful feature extractor within a Prototypical Network framework [15]. Few-shot learning is a paradigm designed to classify new categories from a minimal number of labeled examples, making it uniquely suited for the IVN security domain where
labeled data for novel, “zero-day” attacks is inherently scarce. Through
this work, we demonstrate that a few-shot learning-based approach can
effectively differentiate multiple attack types, overcoming the limitations that hinder traditional supervised methods.

**2.** **Background**


The CAN bus underpins in-vehicle communication by enabling realtime data exchange among multiple ECUs responsible for engine, transmission, and braking. Initially developed by Bosch in the mid-1980s

[2] and introduced in vehicles around 1991, CAN is a multi-master,
message-oriented protocol well-suited to automotive requirements for
efficiency, reliability, and scalability. Each ECU can send and receive
messages, with priority determined by the message identifier (ID): a
lower ID denotes higher priority, ensuring that time-critical messages
are transmitted first during bus contention.


_2.1._ _Controller_ _area_ _network_


A standard CAN 2.0A or 2.0B data frame consists of several fields (see
Fig. 1), but from a security standpoint, the Base Identifier and the Data
Field are of primary importance. The Base Identifier (11 bits) serves a
dual purpose, including determining the message’s priority during bus
arbitration and identifying the message type (e.g., engine RPM, wheel
speed). However, it provides no mechanism for authenticating the identity of the sending ECU. Any node on the bus can send a message with
any ID. Data Field (0-8 bytes) contains the actual payload, such as a
sensor reading or an actuator command. In the standard CAN protocol,
this data is transmitted in plaintext, without any encryption to ensure
confidentiality or cryptographic checksums to guarantee integrity. This
lack of built-in security features, combined with the broadcast nature of
the bus where all nodes receive all messages, creates a permissive environment for attackers. Malicious actors can easily inject, manipulate,
or disrupt critical communications, making the development of a robust
IDS a necessity.


_2.2._ _Problem_ _description_


While CAN’s broadcast design and priority-based arbitration increase
network efficiency, they also present inherent security weaknesses. Because CAN lacks built-in authentication and encryption, attackers can
inject or manipulate messages that appear legitimate to other nodes,
jeopardizing vehicle safety. Malicious nodes can exploit priority arbitration to override lower-priority traffic or flood the bus with high-priority
frames, leading to denial-of-service (DoS) conditions and blocking critical functions.

Once an adversary gains access to the bus, they can disrupt or
hijack ECUs, potentially disabling safety features or issuing spurious
commands. These adversarial strategies are generally categorized into
three primary types [18]:


_•_ Fabrication Attacks: An adversary injects fabricated messages to disrupt vehicle functions. This includes DoS attacks, where the bus is
flooded with high-priority frames to block critical communications,
or fuzzing attacks that inject malformed data to crash ECUs [20].

_•_ Suspension Attacks: A compromised ECU is forced to halt its normal transmissions, thereby withholding vital sensor or status updates
from other nodes in the network. The absence of this expected data
can degrade or disable dependent safety systems.

_•_ Masquerade Attacks: An attacker impersonates a legitimate ECU,
intercepting its normal messages while injecting its own carefully
crafted frames. This allows for stealthy manipulation of vehicle behavior, such as causing unintended acceleration or disabling brakes,
without triggering obvious alarms.


The continuous emergence of new and sophisticated variations of
these attacks poses a significant challenge for traditional, supervised
multi-class IDSs. When a novel or “zero-day” attack is identified, these
conventional models require a full and extensive retraining cycle. This
process is not only resource-intensive but, more critically, is contingent
on the collection of a large, well-labeled dataset for the new attack
class.


In the automotive domain, acquiring such datasets is a major bottleneck. It is often impractical, costly, and time-consuming to generate
and label vast amounts of data for every potential new threat. This data
scarcity problem is precisely where few-shot learning offers a promising
and pragmatic solution. By enabling a model to learn and accurately
identify a new intrusion class from only a handful of examples, fewshot learning eliminates the need for large-scale data collection and retraining. This provides a scalable and adaptive pathway toward building
robust IVN security systems capable of keeping pace with an evolving
threat landscape.


**3.** **Related** **works**


The escalating sophistication of IVN attacks endangers vehicle safety
and security, particularly given the CAN bus’s vulnerabilities, such as
its lack of built-in authentication and encryption. Although cryptographic measures can be introduced, they often degrade network performance [21]. Recognizing these challenges, a large body of work has
shifted its focus to IDSs, which identify malicious behaviors without
necessarily modifying the CAN protocol itself. IDSs are broadly classified into fingerprinting, parameter monitoring, information-theoretic,
and machine-learning approaches [22]. Notably, deep learning-based
IDSs have proven significantly more effective than other alternative approaches. Various IDSs have been proposed to monitor and analyze messages transmitted on the bus, detect malicious behaviors, and issue alerts
when such activities are identified [20].



_3.1._ _Deep_ _learning-based_ _in-vehicle_ _IDS_


Kang and Kang[23] pioneered deep learning for CAN intrusion detection by mapping each payload bit to separate neurons, achieving
98 % accuracy on a synthetic dataset with limited attack scenarios.
Subsequent studies introduced more advanced architectures. For instance, Song et al.[17] converted binary CAN IDs into 29×29 images for
an Inception ResNet, achieving low FNR on DoS/spoofing but weaker
fuzzy-attack detection. Hoang and Kim[11] combined CNNs and autoencoders, efficiently learning compressed representations using only
40 % labeled data and reaching an F1 score of 0.9974. Cheng et al.[24]
extended this approach via sparse regularization and stream clustering,
reporting F1 scores of 0.9634 on the Car Hacking dataset and 0.956 on
the ROAD dataset.


In parallel, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and long short-term
memory networks (LSTMs) have been widely employed to leverage
temporal patterns. Taylor et al.[14] utilized LSTM to predict the next
CAN payload bit to detect anomalies, while Ashraf et al.[25] combined
LSTM-based autoencoder methods, achieving an F1 score up to 0.99 on
the Car Hacking dataset and 0.98 on UNSW-NB15 dataset. Hanselmann
et al.[26] trained one LSTM per CAN ID but required manageable signal
volumes to avoid high resource use. Mansourian et al.[27] used a modified version of LSTM that uses the convolution operation in its structure
to capture spatio-temporal features, pushing accuracy close to 100 % in
the Car Hacking dataset.

Researchers later explored transformer-based architectures [28], effectively handling complex sequential data in IVN security. Nguyen
et al.[12] developed a transformer for high-complexity classification
tasks but reported a high FNR in the Survival dataset. Another approach
employed a bi-directional GPT model on CAN ID sequences [29], yet obtained a relatively low F1 score on the Car Hacking dataset. Although
transformer models show promise, their performance on modern, sophisticated datasets remains underexplored.

Beyond these direct supervised classifiers, other learning paradigms
have been explored to address the challenge of labeled data scarcity. For
instance, GIDS utilized Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to learn
the distribution of normal CAN traffic and thereby detect anomalous
deviations [30]. More recent work has leveraged self-supervised learning to train anomaly detectors on unlabeled data by having the model
learn to distinguish normal traffic from artificially noised versions [31].
While these sophisticated approaches have significantly advanced IVN
security, whether supervised or unsupervised, they predominantly fall
into binary classification schemes that neglect the fine-grained identification of specific attack types. This leaves a critical gap for a system
that can perform robust multi-class classification while simultaneously
minimizing the dependency on large-scale labeled data.

_3.2._ _Paradigms_ _for_ _overcoming_ _data_ _challenges_ _in_ _intrusion_ _detection_


The operational model of traditional supervised learning, which
relies on large, static datasets, is increasingly misaligned with the
dynamic nature of cybersecurity threats. For IDS, particularly in
safety-critical domains like IVNs, the key challenge is adapting to
novel, zero-day attacks without the luxury of extensive labeled data.
This has driven research into more agile, data-efficient learning
paradigms, notably zero-shot learning, federated learning, and few-shot
learning.



Zero-shot learning seeks to identify new attack classes without any
prior training examples. For example, recent studies have applied zeroshot learning to Network IDS by using semantic attributes of known
attacks to build a classifier capable of recognizing novel threats that
share those attributes [35,36]. While innovative, the performance of
zero-shot learning is highly dependent on these attributes, which are
difficult to define for the semantically poor, numerical data of the CAN
bus, making few-shot learning a more practical approach for the IVN

context.


Federated learning is another key paradigm for vehicular networks,
enabling a fleet of vehicles to collaboratively train a robust IDS model
without centralizing private CAN data [34,37]. While powerful for creating a generalized model across a fleet, it mainly focuses on data privacy and distribution, not the rapid, single-system adaptation to unseen
attacks, which remains our core problem and is better addressed by fewshot learning.

To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has reported a multiclass IVN IDS that uses metric-based few-shot learning, though it
has shown significant promise in adjacent network security domains

[38,39]. Few-shot learning directly tackles the problem of data scarcity
by designing models that can generalize effectively from a minimal number of labeled samples. These methods are generally grouped into three
main families: optimization-based approaches like MAML [40], which
learn a highly adaptable model initialization; memory-based approaches
like MANN [41], which use external memory to store knowledge; and
metric-based approaches as Prototypical Networks [15], which learn a
feature space for similarity-based classification.

For the task of building a practical and resource-aware IDS for IVNs,
we selected a metric-based approach for several strategic reasons. First,
they are highly computationally efficient at inference time, as they only
require a simple forward pass and distance calculation, avoiding the
complex optimization steps or memory lookups of other few-shot learning families. This is critical for deployment on resource-constrained
ECUs. Second, the architectural simplicity of models like Prototypical
Networks leads to more stable training and greater interpretability; the
concept of classifying an attack based on its “closeness” to a known attack prototype is intuitive. Third, and critically for a system trained on
limited data, the metric-based paradigm is inherently more robust to
overfitting. Unlike traditional classifiers that can learn to memorize the
specific noise of individual samples, Prototypical Networks are trained
to learn a generalizable metric space. The model’s objective is to create robust class embeddings where attacks are clustered by similarity,
which makes it far less susceptible to overfitting on the small support
set. Finally, this paradigm aligns perfectly with the objective of IDS:
to effectively categorize incoming traffic, including novel threats, into
distinct classes.


Given these advantages, this paper pioneers the application of
metric-based few-shot learning for multi-class IVN IDS. By integrating a
Prototypical Network with a specialized Convolutional Anomaly Transformer, we propose a framework specifically designed for the rapid and
data-efficient adaptation required to secure modern vehicles against an
evolving threat landscape.

_4.2._ _Overview_


The proposed IDS, illustrated in Fig. 2, features a modular architecture designed to process and analyze CAN bus traffic within IVNs
efficiently. The system begins by collecting raw CAN messages, which
are then preprocessed and organized into structured data frames. The
timestamp is converted to a time-based positional encoding and fed
into the transformer at sequence extraction, enabling it to leverage



messages’ temporal order and spacing for more effective pattern and
anomaly detection. These frames undergo feature extraction via 1D convolutional layers (Conv1D), enabling the system to capture critical patterns within individual messages. The processed data are subsequently
passed through a Sequential pattern extraction module powered by a
transformer network, which captures the temporal relationships within
the message sequence. Finally, the resulting embeddings are compared
using similarity measures to classify the sequence as normal or one of
several predefined attack types. This architecture ensures robust, realtime detection and classification of anomalies, enhancing the security
of the vehicle’s network.

_4.5._ _Sequential_ _pattern_ _extraction_ _module_


While the Conv1D block is effective at extracting features from individual messages, many sophisticated attacks, such as DoS floods or



message replay, are only detectable by analyzing the temporal patterns
across a sequence of messages. To capture these complex, long-range
dependencies, we employ an Anomaly Transformer as our sequential
analysis module.

We specifically chose a Transformer-based architecture for its powerful self-attention mechanism [28]. Unlike traditional recurrent models,
self-attention excels at identifying relationships between all messages in
a sequence simultaneously, making it highly effective for modeling the
intricate timing and ordering patterns of CAN bus traffic.

Our model adapts the core concept of the Anomaly Transformer
by identifying anomalies through an “association discrepancy”. This is
the difference between a learned prior-association (which models normal patterns) and the series-association derived directly from the selfattention mechanism. For this task, we utilize only the encoder component of the full Transformer architecture to generate a final, context-rich
representation of the entire message sequence. This approach allows the
model to effectively pinpoint deviations from established normal behavior, flagging them as potential intrusions.


_4.5.1._ _Anomaly_ _attention_

To effectively identify anomalies, our model utilizes the Anomaly Attention mechanism proposed by [16], which is a significant extension of
the standard self-attention found in the original Transformer architecture. Standard self-attention is highly effective at capturing the global
relationships in a sequence by calculating a series-association score for
all data points. However, for anomaly detection, relying solely on these
global patterns can be insufficient. Anomaly Attention improves upon
this by introducing a second, competing component: a prior-association
that assumes normal data points have stronger connections to their immediate neighbors. The core innovation is the Association Discrepancy
between these two learned associations (the global series-association
and the local prior-association). As illustrated in Fig. 5, by training the

_4.7._ _Few-shot_ _learning_ _with_ _prototypical_ _network_


Given the inherent scarcity of labeled data for emerging threats, fewshot learning is a natural paradigm for IVN security. We specifically
select a Prototypical Network as our foundation due to its computational efficiency and its intuitive approach of classifying threats based
on feature-space similarity—a strong fit for both resource-constrained
ECUs and the anomaly detection task. The success of any metricbased few-shot learning, however, is critically dependent on the quality of the feature space it operates on. Our primary contribution,
therefore, lies not in altering the Prototypical Network itself, but
in designing a novel feature extractor—the Convolutional Anomaly
Transformer—that generates uniquely powerful embeddings for this
task. As detailed previously, this encoder is specifically architected
to capture both fine-grained intra-message details and broader sequential patterns, creating a rich, context-aware representation of the
CAN traffic. By applying the Prototypical Network to these highly
discriminative embeddings, our model can effectively and efficiently
generalize from minimal examples to classify diverse IVN attacks. The
entire process, illustrated in Fig. 6, is driven by an episodic training
strategy designed to teach the model how to learn from new tasks on the

_4.7.1._ _Episodic_ _training_ _and_ _task_ _creation_ _framework_

A traditional supervised training approach would optimize the model
for a fixed set of known attacks. This method fundamentally fails in a
real-world security context, as the model would be unable to handle
novel, zero-day threats without a complete and costly retraining cycle.
To overcome this limitation, we employ **episodic** **training**, a paradigm
that shifts the objective from _learning_ _to_ _classify_ to _learning_ _how_ _to_ _learn_ .

Instead of training on the entire dataset at once, our framework trains
the model on a continuous stream of unique “episodes” or tasks. This
approach teaches the model to become a flexible, few-shot classifier on
the fly. We designed a custom task creation mechanism that constructs
these episodes to mimic the challenge of encountering a new attack:


1. **Task** **Sampling:** At each training step, our sampler randomly selects

_𝑁_ way distinct attack classes from the available training pool.
2. **Support** **&** **Query** **Set** **Construction:** For these _𝑁_ classes, it samples

_𝐾_ shot examples per class to form the **support** **set** (the “few-shots”
for learning) and a separate batch of examples to form the **query** **set**
(for evaluation).
3. **Out-of-Distribution** **(OOD)** **Sampling:** Crucially, our mechanism
extends beyond the standard framework. To power our novel dualloss objective (detailed in the next section), the sampler also selects
a set of OOD samples—attacks from classes that are not part of the
current _𝑁_ -way task.


The configuration for each dataset, shown in Table 2, was chosen
strategically. For the Car Hacking dataset, we set _𝑁_ way = 4to utilize all
available classes; for the more diverse ROAD dataset, we use _𝑁_ way = 5
to provide varied but manageable learning tasks; and for the CAN-ML
dataset, we chose _𝑁_ way = 7 to encompass all of its distinct attack classes.
The impact of this is analyzed in our ablation studies (Section 5.4.1).

By training on thousands of these diverse, dynamically generated
episodes, the model does not merely memorize a few attack signatures.
Instead, it learns a robust, metric-based feature space where it can compute a class prototype from any new set of _𝐾_ support samples and

_5.1._ _Dataset_ _selection_


To ensure a comprehensive and rigorous evaluation, we selected
three distinct and progressively challenging datasets. All datasets provide the same fundamental CAN message features—a timestamp, CAN
ID, and an 8-byte data payload—making them structurally comparable
for a fair assessment. Our multi-faceted evaluation strategy is as follows: first, we establish a performance baseline on a standard benchmark to ensure comparability with existing literature; second, we test
the model’s robustness against stealthy attacks and severe imbalance;
and finally, we validate its generalization capabilities on a large-scale,
highly diverse, and realistic dataset.


_5.1.1._ _Car_ _hacking_ _dataset:_ _A_ _benchmark_ _for_ _comparability_

The Car Hacking dataset [17] is a foundational benchmark in IVN
security research. Recorded in a real IVN via the OBD-II port during
controlled message injection attacks, it contains four well-defined attack
types: DoS, Fuzzy, RPM Spoofing, and Gear Spoofing. Table 3 provides
an overview of the Car Hacking dataset by displaying the distribution
of normal versus injected messages for various attack types. The dataset
exhibits a moderate class imbalance, which provides a controlled environment for initial evaluation. Due to its widespread adoption, this
dataset serves as an essential baseline, allowing for a direct comparison
of our model’s performance against a large body of existing work in the
field.


_5.1.2._ _Road_ _dataset:_ _Testing_ _robustness_ _to_ _imbalance_ _and_ _stealthy_ _attacks_

Unlike the Car Hacking dataset’s basic attacks, the ROAD dataset

[18] is one of the most representative and comprehensive benchmarks
for evaluating IVN intrusion detection. Collected using SocketCAN connected to an actual vehicle’s OBD-II port on a rolling dynamometer,
ROAD provides authentic attack scenarios rather than relying on synthetic or post-processed data. Unlike the Car Hacking dataset, ROAD
introduces two critical challenges not fully represented in the Car Hacking dataset. First, it includes more advanced and stealthy masquerade
(MAS) attacks in addition to basic fabrication (FAB) attacks. Second, as
shown in Table 4, it reflects a severe class imbalance, where malicious
messages often constitute less than 3 % of the traffic. The inclusion of
stealthy attack types and their pronounced data imbalance makes ROAD
a crucial testbed for evaluating our model’s robustness and its ability to
detect rare anomalies, a common real-world scenario.


_5.1.3._ _CAN-ML_ _dataset:_ _Validating_ _generalization_ _with_ _diverse_ _attacks_

The recent CAN-ML corpus [19], captured from a modern vehicle, is
our final and most rigorous test. Its primary contribution is attack diversity. For this study, we selected the 2017 Subaru Forester subset because
it offers the newest vehicle model with a combination of long-duration
recordings and attack variety. As detailed in Table 5, it contains nine
distinct and modern attack styles, including timing-based floods and
systematic attacks, recorded over long-duration sessions. This variety,

_5.1.4._ _Data_ _preparation_ _and_ _partitioning_

After processing CAN messages, each resulting data instance is subsequently labeled as either “benign” or a specific “attack” type (e.g., DoS,
Fuzzy, etc.). For each attack scenario, we then drew a stratified 10 %
sample that maintained the same proportion of benign and malicious
frames as the full scenario. These samples were subsequently merged
across all scenarios to form the pool used for training. The remaining
90 % of instances from all scenarios are further divided into a 10 % val
idation set and an 80 % test set. This 10 %–10 %–80 % (train-validatetest) partitioning, derived from the stratified sampling of processed CAN
message data, was consistently applied across all experiments. This approach facilitates robust model development and hyperparameter tuning on limited yet representative data, while ensuring a substantial and
diverse test set for a rigorous final evaluation of model generalization.
This limited training portion showcases how a few-shot learning approach can still achieve robust results while minimizing the amount of
data needed.

_5.4.2._ _K-shot_ _experimentation_

In few-shot learning, the value of _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ (the number of examples per
class) significantly impacts the model’s effectiveness. A smaller _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_
may not provide the model with sufficient samples to effectively distinguish between classes, potentially leading to a higher FNR and reduced
overall performance. Conversely, a large _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ can cause overfitting, diminishing generalizability and leading to over-specialization on the specific training examples.

To evaluate the influence of _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ on our model, we conducted experiments with _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ = 3 _,_ 5 _,_ 10 _,_ 15 _,_ 20 using the ROAD fabrication dataset.
These experiments employed a 5-way 5-shot learning configuration with
64-dimensional embeddings, training the model exclusively on 10 % of
the ROAD fabrication dataset, as outlined previously, while the remaining 90 % was reserved for testing. The results, presented in Fig. 8, reveal
stable performance across _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ values, with FNR ranging from 0.28 % to
0.41 %, recall from 0.9959 to 0.9972, precision from 0.9740 to 0.9893,
and F1 scores from 0.9849 to 0.9933. The 5-shot configuration stands
out as the optimal choice, achieving the lowest FNR (0.28 %), the highest recall (0.9972), the highest precision (0.9893), and the highest F1
score (0.9933). In contrast, larger _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ values like 20-shot exhibit a
decline in recall (0.9959) and an increased FNR (0.41 %), indicating
potential overfitting, while 10-shot (FNR 0.30 %, F1 0.9920) performs
slightly less effectively.

The results confirm that _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ = 5 provides an ideal balance, offering sufficient examples to minimize FNR and maximize F1 score while
maintaining robust generalizability. This experiment highlights the critical role of _𝐾_ _𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑡_ selection in few-shot learning for IVN security, with the
ROAD fabrication dataset serving as a reliable platform for optimizing
this parameter.

that better represent real-world IVN conditions. Unlike the Car Hacking

ROAD dataset encompasses a broader spectrum of sophisticated intrusions, providing a more challenging and comprehensive benchmark for
evaluating IDS models.

To validate the effectiveness of our backbone model in a few-shot

learning context, we conducted additional experiments using different
backbone architectures to demonstrate the robustness of our proposed
approach. These experiments were meticulously crafted to showcase the

dataset. The backbone architectures evaluated in our experiments en
compass:


_•_ **ResNet18** : A well-established deep learning architecture known for
its effectiveness in various image and signal processing tasks. We
included ResNet as a strong comparative model to evaluate how our
approach measures up against a widely recognized backbone.

_•_ **ConvNet** : A 4-layer convolutional model originally proposed in the
studies of Prototypical Networks [15]. This model serves as a baseline for comparison, representing a standard approach in few-shot
learning tasks.

_•_ **Anomaly** **Transformer** (AT): In this variant, we removed the initial Conv1D layers from our proposed model to assess the impact of
these layers on performance. This version retains the transformer architecture and focuses solely on the sequential data without initial
convolutional feature extraction.

few-shot scenario, while the remaining 90 % was set aside for testing.

competitive performance, ensuring robust intrusion detection without

_5.7._ _Ablation_ _experiments_


_5.7.1._ _Component-wise_ _architectural_ _ablation_

To validate our architectural design and quantify the contribution of
each component, we performed an ablation study by systematically evaluating different model configurations. The results, illustrated in Fig. 14,
reveal complementary performance patterns on the two datasets that
justify our final hybrid design.

As shown in the figure, on the ROAD dataset—where attacks
are temporally stealthy—the global context captured by attention is
decisive. The “Attention Only” model significantly outperforms the
“Conv1D Only” baseline, lifting the F1 score from 0.9442 to 0.9765.
Combining Conv1D with attention creates a powerful synergy, boosting
the F1 score to 0.9918. The final addition of our lightweight time encoding provides a further gain to 0.9933, driven primarily by a large
increase in precision. This demonstrates that for masquerade-style attacks, long-range dependencies are key, and explicit timing information
helps reduce false alarms. In contrast, the results on the CAN-ML dataset
highlight the brittleness of attention when faced with more varied manipulations and class imbalance. The “Attention Only” model collapses
(F1 score 0.7001), whereas the “Conv1D Only” model provides a solid
baseline (0.9464). The hybrid “Conv1D + Attention” model resolves
this, achieving an F1 score of 0.9750. Once again, adding time encoding delivers the best overall performance, reaching our highest F1 score
of 0.9918 by substantially improving both precision and recall.

Together, these results justify the final stack: Conv1D captures local byte-pattern evidence, attention enforces sequence-level consistency
against temporally stealthy attacks, and lightweight time encoding stabilizes decisions under cadence drift. The same configuration achieves
the best trade-off on both benchmarks by raising recall on ROAD and
precision on CAN-ML, thereby meeting IVN IDS requirements to minimize both missed detections and false alarms while generalizing beyond
a single dataset.

_5.7.3._ _Ablation_ _study_ _of_ _the_ _Conv1D_ _layer_

To determine the optimal configuration for the Conv1D block, we
performed three parameter sweeps, evaluating the trade-offs between
detection performance and model efficiency. The results, visualized in
Fig. 16, decisively justify our final design choices.

First, our analysis of filter quantity identifies a clear accuracyefficiency frontier at 32 filters. As shown in the figure, increasing from
16 to 32 filters significantly boosts the F1 score from 0.9882 to 0.9933
by reducing false alarms (precision rises from 0.9790 to 0.9893). While
using 64 filters matches this peak F1 score, it does so with a 23 %
larger memory footprint (0.75 MB vs. 0.61 MB) for no performance gain.
Further increasing to 128 filters degrades performance while adding
substantial cost. Therefore, 32 filters are the optimal choice, achieving maximum accuracy at the most efficient resource cost. Second, the
sweep over kernel size confirms that a size of 3 provides the best feature
resolution. A kernel size of 3 achieves the highest F1 score (0.9933). As
the figure illustrates, larger kernels (5 and 7) begin to erode precision,
causing the F1 score to drop. This indicates that wider receptive fields
start to blur the distinct byte-level patterns of attack signatures, leading
to more false positives. We thus select a kernel size of 3 to maintain
the highest precision and overall F1 score without adding any memory
overhead. Finally, our experiments show that increasing network depth
is counterproductive in this architecture. Adding a second Conv1D layer
degrades the F1 score to 0.9872, and three layers fail to match the performance of a single layer, all while incrementally increasing model size
and latency. This strongly suggests that a single Conv1D layer is sufficient to capture local evidence, and that deeper convolutional stacks
begin to overfit or become redundant given the global context already
provided by the attention mechanism.

In sum, the chosen configuration—one Conv1D layer with 32 filters
and kernel size 3—sits on the accuracy-efficiency frontier: it attains the
top F1 while reducing footprint and preserving high recall. We therefore

_5.7.4._ _Ablation_ _study_ _of_ _the_ _attention_ _layer_

To finalize the architecture of our Anomaly Transformer, we performed a hyperparameter sweep to optimize the number of encoder layers and attention heads, balancing detection performance with computational cost. The results, visualized in Fig. 17, clearly justify our choice
of a 10-layer, 10-head configuration. The analysis of encoder depth (left
chart) reveals a distinct performance peak at 10 layers. Increasing from
6 to 10 layers yields a substantial gain in the F1 score (from 0.985 to
0.993), driven by a sharp improvement in precision. However, increasing further to 12 layers causes the F1 score to decline, while the memory
footprint continues to grow. This indicates that 10 layers provide the
optimal depth to capture temporal dependencies, while a deeper stack



begins to overfit or offers diminishing returns at a higher resource cost.
A similar “sweet spot” is evident in the analysis of the number of attention heads (right chart). The F1 score again peaks at 10 heads, reaching
0.993. Using only 6 heads results in a lower F1 score, while increasing to 12 heads not only degrades performance but also significantly
increases the model’s memory footprint from 0.75 MB to 1.0 MB. This
demonstrates that 10 heads provide the ideal balance of representational

capacity and efficiency for this task.


**6.** **Discussion**


_6.1._ _Feasibility_ _of_ _real-world_ _automotive_ _deployment_


The proposed IDS was intentionally designed with production ECUs
in mind. Its encoder requires only **0.16** **M** **parameters** and **2.03** **M**



19


_N.T.M._ _Duy_ _et_ _al._


**multiply-accumulates** **(MACs)**, which is ∼4× lighter than SupCon
ResNet and ∼120× lighter than a vanilla Transformer. The inference
process involves only a forward pass and a simple distance calculation,
making it well-suited for the resource-constrained environments of automotive ECUs. The feasibility of its application in production vehicles
can be assessed from several perspectives:


_•_ **Gateway** **IDS.** Because the encoder is both compact and stateless, it
can reside on a central CAN/Ethernet gateway to protect multiple
bus segments without exceeding AUTOSAR Classic timing budgets.

_•_ **Over-the-air** **(OTA)** **adaptation.** Few-shot finetuning means only a
handful of labelled frames are transmitted OTA, minimizing bandwidth and eliminating the need to flash an entire new model.

_•_ **Fleet-wide** **threat** **sharing.** A cloud backend can aggregate prototypes learned on individual vehicles and redistribute them, offering
federated and privacy-preserving hardening against zero-day attacks
detected in the wild.


_•_ **Predictive** **maintenance.** Anomalous prototypes that recur—yet are
not linked to any known attack—can serve as early indicators of
failing sensors/actuators, extending the IDS beyond security appli
cations.


_6.2._ _Perspectives_ _on_ _scalability_ _in_ _intrusion_ _detection_


A critical attribute for any IDS is scalability, which can be viewed
from multiple perspectives. For instance, approaches like swarm intelligence, as exemplified by the ASRL framework for large-scale social
networks [43], address architectural scalability—the ability to handle
more nodes and data throughput. In contrast, our work targets a different but equally critical dimension: knowledge scalability. Our few-shot
learning framework is specifically designed to efficiently adapt to new,
unseen threats with minimal data, a crucial capability for the dynamic
IVN threat landscape. This is demonstrated by our model’s high performance using as little as 2 % of the training data, showcasing a form of
scalability centered on data efficiency rather than system size. While
distinct, these two forms of scalability are complementary and could be
integrated in future comprehensive security architectures.


_6.3._ _Limitations_ _and_ _mitigation_ _strategies_


While our results are promising, we acknowledge several limitations
that offer valuable directions for future research:


_•_ **Dependence** **on** **a** **few** **labelled** **shots.** Even though the method
thrives with _𝐾_ =5 samples, it still requires some expertly labelled data
per new attack. Semi-supervised augmentation (e.g., consistency regularisation) and active-learning query strategies are promising complements.

_•_ **Handling** **of** **Concept** **Drift.** The current model assumes a static distribution for both normal and attack-class data. However, a vehicle’s
normal traffic patterns may evolve over its lifespan due to component wear or software updates. Future work should explore continual or online learning mechanisms to adapt the prototypes over time,
preventing performance degradation from this natural drift.

_•_ **Adversarial** **robustness.** Adversaries could craft payloads that move
embeddings toward the benign prototype. Integrating adversarial
training or randomized smoothing is a viable defense, as is runtime
ensemble voting across multiple distance metrics.

_•_ **Explainability.** While anomaly-attention offers some interpretability, field engineers still need frame-level root-cause traces. Coupling
the IDS with Explainable-AI post-hoc saliency maps is therefore an
essential next step.


_6.4._ _Future_ _research_ _roadmap_


Building on the above, we envision three concrete research direc
tions:



_Knowledge-Based_ _Systems_ _330_ _(2025)_ _114436_


1. **Investigating** **True** **Domain-Agnostic** **Signatures.** A significant future research challenge is extending our model to a true zero-shot,
cross-vendor transfer learning scenario. This is a non-trivial task, as it
requires overcoming the fundamental domain shift in baseline CAN
traffic between different vehicle manufacturers (e.g., Toyota vs. Subaru). Furthermore, it would involve a rigorous process of harmonizing attack labels to ensure a DoS attack in one dataset is semantically equivalent to another. This likely requires developing specific
domain adaptation techniques to build upon the foundational model
presented in this work.
2. **Continual** **on-vehicle** **learning.** Use lightweight memory buffers
to store representative examples and avoid catastrophic forgetting
when prototypes are updated in place.
3. **Automated** **Architecture** **Optimization.** Our current architecture
was manually designed based on established principles. A promising
next step is to employ Neural Architecture Search techniques [44] to
automatically discover potentially more potent or resource-efficient
network designs, further optimizing the trade-offs between detection
accuracy and on-board computational constraints.


**7.** **Conclusion**


This study introduces a novel IDS for IVN security using the CAN bus,
marking the first application of few-shot learning in this domain. Our
contributions include the development of a lightweight architecture, the
Convolutional Anomaly Transformer, which effectively integrates convolutional layers and transformers to capture both spatial and temporal characteristics of CAN data, enabling robust and accurate intrusion
detection. Comprehensive experiments on the Car Hacking and ROAD
datasets demonstrate the strong performance of the proposed model. On
the ROAD dataset, our approach achieved F1 scores of 0.9933 for fabrication attacks and 0.9980 for masquerade attacks, with FNRs of 0.32 %
and 0.04 %, respectively, across attack types such as CS, MS, and RLOn.
On the Car Hacking dataset, the model attained an F1 score of 0.9994
using only 2 % of the data for training, highlighting its efficiency with
limited data. These results, which surpass typical F1 scores of 0.95–0.97
reported in prior IVN security studies, underscore the effectiveness of
our approach in enhancing automotive cybersecurity, particularly for
resource-constrained in-vehicle systems.

Future research can further enhance the model’s capabilities by testing it across a broader range of attack scenarios and incorporating zeroshot learning techniques, such as generative adversarial networks, to
detect unknown or evolving threats. Additionally, integrating Explainable AI (XAI) methods could provide deeper insights into the model’s
decision-making process, improving transparency and trust in its predictions for IVN security applications. Overall, this study establishes a
promising foundation for adaptive and resilient IDS solutions, capable
of safeguarding modern vehicles against a wide range of cybersecurity
threats.
